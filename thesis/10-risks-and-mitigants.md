#### Risk: Recycling Increases

**Probability:** 10-15% (cemented carbide ceiling); 5-10% (chemicals — chemistry-bound)
**Impact:** -10-20% (cemented carbide); -0% (chemicals — 5% rate is structural)
**Mitigation:** Industry-specific recycling rates show most of the 28-44 kt/yr primary demand floor cannot be recycled away

> **v10.1 REFRAMING (AUDIT-2, July 18, 2026):** The earlier "Recycling Increases" risk used a single global recycling rate / blended ceiling framing. The correct framing is **industry-specific** — recycling rates differ vastly by end-use. The "global 30% recycling rate" is a weighted average that masks huge variance:
>
> - **Cemented carbide (60-65% of demand):** 46% rate (cutting tools 55%, mining/wear 40%) — Sandvik 90% buyback target, Plansee 91% mature closed-loop. **Already near ceiling; limited additional supply from recycling growth.**
> - **Steels/superalloys (14-18% of demand):** 15% rate (W is 1-10% of alloy, lost to slag). **Pyrometallurgical losses are structural; cannot be closed.**
> - **Mill products / WHA (12% of demand):** 22% rate (WHA is 90-95% W — economic to recycle).
> - **Chemicals (9-12% of demand):** 5% rate — WF6 chemistry consumed (WF6 + 3H2 → W on wafer + 6HF, gas-phase recovery max 5-10%); SCR/AMT 5%, WS2 <5%, pigments 0%. **7-15× harder to recycle than cemented carbide.**
> - **Other (2-3% of demand):** 5% rate.
>
> **Blended: ~30%** (matches reality). The "practical blended ceiling by 2030: 35-40%" earlier draft was directionally right but lacked the industry decomposition that shows **where the ceiling is binding and where it is NOT**.

**Details:**
- Tungsten scrap is valuable **but only in specific end-uses (cemented carbide, WHA)**
- Recycling is **mature** in cemented carbide (Sandvik/Plansee/Ceratizit already at 88-91%) — additional supply from recycling growth is small
- **The 28-44 kt/yr "permanent primary demand floor"** = industries where recycling CANNOT close the gap (chemistry-bound chemicals + dispersed end-uses + pyrometallurgical losses)
- TGN's 1,657 t/yr W is 4-6% of the 28-44 kt/yr floor — small slice, but the **only Tier 2 RDP primary producer ramping Q2 2027** (US Tier 1 mines TUNGF Phase II Q2 2027 + Blue Moon Q4 2027 close the US DoD window by Q4 2027; civilian APT + Japan 6N + allied defense thesis continues)
- The structural APT price floor is **NOT** threatened by recycling because:
  - Cemented carbide's 46% is already near ceiling
  - Chemicals' 5% is chemistry-bound (cannot rise meaningfully)
  - Steels' 15% is pyrometallurgy-bound (cannot rise meaningfully)

**Mitigation Strategy:**
- **Cemented carbide recycling has hit its ceiling** (Sandvik/Plansee 90%+ rates, growth marginal) — no further 5-10% supply shock
- **Chemicals' 5% rate is structural** (WF6 + H2 → W + HF, gas-phase only, 7-15× harder than metal recycling) — chemistry prevents the rate from rising
- **Defense demand uses virgin material primarily** (DFARS-compliant scrap is limited)
- **Structural floor remains** at US$2,500-3,000/MTU APT for primary supply because the **permanent primary demand floor** (28-44 kt/yr) cannot be closed by recycling
- **TGN's blended target $2,500-3,100/MTU (4-layer baseline, v5.2 §5f/5g (v5.2 LIVE; 4-layer pricing stack with anti-weaponization)) is supported by**, not threatened by, industry-specific recycling dynamics. Stress-test $3,000-4,300/MTU (if China weaponizes) is also recycling-immune. Tier 2 RDP premium = $400-700/MTU (TIME-BOUNDED 2027-2028 only); Layer 4 anti-weaponization premium = $300-700/MTU (conditional). Customer mix (Mt Mulgine 6 Mtpa) underpins the 4-layer baseline: 60% civilian APT (durable, 20-30 kt/yr ex-China addressable) + 20% Japan 6N/WF6 (durable) + 15% allied defense non-US (durable) + 5% US DoD captive (12-18 month window). The TIME-BOUNDED 2027-2028 caveat applies only to the 5% US DoD captive; the 95% durable mix (60/20/15) is the structural floor that does not collapse when US Tier 1 mines ramp Q4 2027.

**The reframed risk:** "Recycling Increases" only threatens **cemented carbide supply** (TGN not in that segment) and **WHA supply** (TGN partial exposure). The 28-44 kt/yr structural floor where TGN plays is **recycling-immune** by chemistry and physics.

**Cross-references to Section 5n + 5q (see `thesis/v10-clean-executive.md` lines 944-1674 for full content):**

- If 77% recovery rate drops to 60%, or if penalty elements (phosphorus, fluorine) degrade payability, Mt Mulgine NPV compresses materially
- Penalty elements (P, F, As) can reduce payability by 5-15% in scheelite concentrates
- Polymetallic separation adds capital + operating costs

**Quantitative impact:**
- Baseline: 77% WO₃ recovery, 65-75% payability, multi-metal credits
- Stress: 60% WO₃ recovery, 50-60% payability, no Mo credit
- Mt Mulgine NPV: A$358-495M → A$250-350M (-A$100-150M)
- Per share impact: -A$0.05-0.10

**Specific risks:**
- **Q2/Q3 2026 Updated PFS:** Metallurgical testwork is the key catalyst
- **Phosphorus penalty:** If P > 0.05%, scheelite concentrate payability drops
- **Fluorine penalty:** If F > 0.1%, environmental + processing costs increase
- **Mo-W separation:** Difficult in porphyry systems; can result in 20-30% Mo loss

**Mitigation Strategy:**
- Watch Q2/Q3 2026 Updated PFS metallurgical testwork results closely
- If 77% recovery holds + multi-metal credits work: Mt Mulgine NPV A$358-495M (Base case)
- If 60% recovery + no Mo credit: Mt Mulgine NPV A$250-350M (Bear case)
- Watershed 76 kt MRE (per June 18, 2026 PEE) doesn't depend on Mt Mulgine metallurgy; Hatches Creek 20.9 kt (high-grade narrow-vein wolframite, 0.17% WO₃) is upside only
